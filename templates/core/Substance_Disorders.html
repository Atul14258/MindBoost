About Substance Disorders

Substance-related and addictive disorders refer to a group of conditions characterized by the harmful consequences of repeated use of substances, including alcohol and illicit drugs. These disorders can have significant negative impacts on an individual's physical health, mental well-being, and overall quality of life. Here are some key points about substance-related and addictive disorders

Definition: Substance-related and addictive disorders are a class of mental health disorders outlined in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5). These disorders encompass a range of conditions related to the use of psychoactive substances. Types of Substances: The substances involved in these disorders can include alcohol, nicotine, cannabis, hallucinogens, opioids, stimulants, and other substances with the potential for abuse. Diagnostic Criteria: The DSM-5 outlines specific criteria for diagnosing substance-related and addictive disorders. Diagnosis is based on factors such as impaired control over substance use, social impairment, risky use, and pharmacological criteria (tolerance and withdrawal)

Severity Levels: The severity of substance-related and addictive disorders is classified as mild, moderate, or severe, based on the number of criteria met. Withdrawal: Withdrawal refers to the physiological and psychological symptoms that occur when a person reduces or discontinues substance use after prolonged and heavy consumption. Withdrawal symptoms can vary depending on the substance. Tolerance: Tolerance is the need for increasing amounts of a substance to achieve the desired effect or a diminished effect with continued use of the same amount

It's important to note that seeking professional help is essential for the assessment, diagnosis, and treatment of substance-related and addictive disorders. If you or someone you know is struggling with substance abuse, contacting a healthcare professional or addiction treatment center is recommended.
